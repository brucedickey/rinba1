How To Play
RULES
  1. Take turns moving one piece as far as it can go in a stright line in any open direction without moving over another piece or occupying a square already taken. You may move between diagonally-adjacent pieces. To move a piece, click it (a "+" is added to it) and then the destination square. Legal destination squares are highlighted with a light green target.
  2. To win, position your pieces so that no two are adjacent. For example, pieces on squares a1 and b2 would be adjacent, but pieces on a1 and a3 would not be.
  3. It is illegal to use your pieces to surround an unoccupied corner square and you will not be permitted to move into that position. For example, placing your pieces on squares a2, b2, and b1 would surround corner square a1. The reason for this rule is that without it a draw could be forced by always moving your remaining fourth piece. It is permitted that a mixture of blue and orange pieces surround an unocupied corner square.
GAME PLAY

In this implementation, two persons take turns playing. Play against the computer is envisioned for a later version. The blue pieces always move first.

Either you or the computer can move first. You will play the blue circles, and the computer will use the orange squares.

  • If you want to move first, click a blue circle and then the destination square.
  • If you want the computer to move first, click the Force Computer Move button.

If at any time it is the computer's move and you want it to move immediately, click the Force Computer Move button.

To reset/abort the game (whether it is finished or not), click the Reset Game button.

About Rinba v1.0
INTRODUCTION

The name "Rinba" is a mash-up of "rings" and "balls" -- wooden ones were used as the first physical game board locations and playing pieces. As you can see, this game can be played on a quarter of a checker-board, or at a camp site using stones.

I invented Rinba in 2006 and implemented it on the Zillions platform which may be purchased for $24.98 (Rinba not included). If you take a look, the Zillions engine takes a .zrf (zillions rules file) written in Lisp. For Rinba this is a very short file.
